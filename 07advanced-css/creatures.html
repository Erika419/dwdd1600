Yara-Ya-Ma-Who

Description

The Yara-Ya-Ma-Who is a vampiric monster from Aborigonal Australians mythology. It's most common depiction is a four-foot tall red figure with a giant mouth, pot belly, skinny arms and legs. It was said to reside in fig trees where it would wait for prey (especially children) to come by and ambush it. Yet unlike other monsters, it didn't have razor sharp teeth or claws to kill it's victims. Instead, it had suckers on it's hands and feet that it used to drain it's prey of blood. Once the prey went unconcious, it would open its mouth freakishly wide and swallow its victim whole, like a snake. Drained of energy, it would go to the nearest watering hole to take a nap and then vomit it's prey. If the victim escapes, it slowly turns into a Yara-Ya-Ma-Who. If not, then it is officially a meal. What is the worse fate? I will let you decide.

Most famous Myth

Due to an inability to find any specific myths about this monster, I am going to explain how people protected themselves, instead. The Yara-Ya-Ma-Who is only able to leave the fig tree during the day and will only ambush living prey. So, if you happen to be in a situation where a fig tree is your only form of shelter, play dead until nightfall and then flee.

Cultural Significance

This creature was, and still is, important to Aborigonals because it offered a lesson. It established rules about where and when to sit, sleep, or gather fruit. This is important because fig branches fallS very easily and figs are only in season during certain months. As a concequence, this myth helps people avoid sleeping/travelling through dangerous areas and avoids overharvesting figs. Therefore, keeping people safe and avoiding ecological damage.

Water Babies

Description

Water Babies are ghostly monsters from the Great Basin region of the US. They reside in springs, lakes, hidden streams, and deep pools. They make an infant-like cry to lure unsuspecting people to the waters edge and then drag them to the bottom, never to be seen again. They are often blamed for mysterious drownings and are viewed as gaurdians of the spirit/water world and the physical one. Granted, beliefs about them vary amongst different tribes. Some traditions claim that Water Babies create storms, floods, or the sudden upsurge of springs when angered. Others claim that their cry is a sign that someone will die soon. Lastly, some claim that they are the spirits of drowned/abandonned babies who's grief has turned them into vengeful spirits. Also, it must be noted that their appearance changes greatly from different tribes. Some describe Water Babies as small human infants with large, dark eyes, wet black hair, and pale skin, and an elongated body. Others describe them as human-fish hybrids, with fins, gills, scaly skin, and long eel-like tails. Others claim that they do not have a physical presence. Instead, they can only be seen as ripples, shadows, or fleeting shapes below the surface. Lastly, others believe them to be shape-shifting spirits that can appear as a baby crying on a rock, a small humanoid swimming swiftly under water, or a drowned child seeking company.

Most famous myths

Long ago there was a Pauite woman who had two children. She took her children down to the river to bathe them but they were dragged under the current. When she discovered her children’s death, she took them to a burial sight to bury them. She cried so hard over her children’s grave that it filled with water and became pyramid lake. However, the woman could not bear to leave her children and laid by the lake to be with them. Overwhelmed by her grief and sorrow, she could not move, eat, sleep, or drink. Eventually, she slowly turned to stone, becoming the Pyramid-like pillar at the edge of the lake. Enraged by this abandonement, the babies became vengeful spirits. To this day her babies can be heard in the lake as they drag unsuspecting victims down to drown them.

Cultural Significance

This creature was, and still is, important to the Great Basin peoples because of the symbolism and lessons it offered. It taught communities to stay safe by staying away from deep pools, avoiding mysterious sounds, and respecting water spirits by being considerate of their homes. It also symbolized the consequences of neglect, in the parental and ecological sense. At the same time, this symbolism implies that each body of water has its own personality. Thereby, binding people to their local environment, preserving ecological knowledge and spiritual geography.

El Silbon

Description

El Silbon is a ghostly monster from the llanos region of Venezuela. He is 9 feet tall, wears a Sombrero hat, has a skinny, slumped frame with freakishly long arms and legs. He stalk the night carrying a machete and a sack of bones slumped on his back. You can tell when he is near by listenening to his haunting whistle or witnessing his glowing eyes. He typically travels around roads, hunting drunks, partiers, and womanizers. However, monsters are made, not born, and El Silbon is no exception. Before his death, he was a spoiled, rich teenager living with his parents and grandparents in a small ranching community. One day, he ordered his father to hunt a deer and when he came back empty handed, the teenager killed his father. Eventually, the grandfather heard of this. He decided to punish the teenager by beating him with a whip, putting chiles and lime juice in his wounds, then feeding him to the dogs. In his dying moments, the grandfather cursed him to forever carry his fathers bones.

Most famous Myth

There was once a rich rancher who threw a party at his home. One of his guests came at midnight, crawling with the last inch of his life. He had messy gashes all along his body with blood pouring out of his wounds. Everyone rushed to help him and ask what happenned. As they tended to his wounds, the man told his tale. He was on his horse, riding across the llano when he realized he was lost. The sun was setting quickly and he seemed to be trapped in an endless abyss of grass. He was quickly stuck in the dark, with only the cold and the sound of wind to keep him company. Suddenly, he heard a strange whistling noise. Thinking that he was hearing a fellow traveler, he called for help. The whistling stopped and was replaced with a strange rattling noise that got louder and louder. He felt a bolt of fear go through him and attempted to slowly back away, hoping to dissapear into the darkness. Suddenly, he bumped into something cold and hard. He turned around and was met with two glowing eyes staring down at him. Before having a chance to scream, the menacing figure attacked him with a machete. He was dealt blow after blow, wondering if anyone would even be able to recognize him after he died. However, the horse hit the stranger as hard as it could, saving its owner. The man used the last of his energy to get back on his horse and rade to safety. After hearing this tale, everyone crossed themselves. "That was El Silbon. Your lucky your alive." They told him. The man thanked them and never rode at night again.

Cultural Significance

El Silbon is a very unique monster whos legend was inspired by a man named Joaquinn Flores in the 1850's. Flores lived in a close-knit ranching community and commited a crime very similar to that of El Silbon. The stigma of this event led to the story morphing into a horrifying and cautionary tale. Yet on a larger scale, his existence reflects Spanish colonialism of Venezuela. The Spanish brought cattle to the llanos region, replacing indigenous groups with Spanish, catholic ranchers. El Silbon reflects this by wearing a Sombrero hat and because he was initially a wealthy ranchers son. Also, the people he hunts goes against spanish, catholic values such as lust (womanizers) and gluttony (drunkars, partygoers).

Zombie

Description

Folklore Zombies are very different from the Zombies in pop culture. They originated from the Haitaion Voudou religion. In Voudou beliefs, there is the ti-bon-anj and the gros-bon-anj. The ti-bon-anj is the part of a person that can contact laos(spirits), personality, free will, and concioiusness. The gros-bon-anj controls the motor functions. After death, it takes several days to fade away. however, a malicious voudou priest called a bokor will trap the ti-bon-anj in a bottle and use it to control the person. They typically turn their victims into mindless workers who do not sleep, eat, or have emotions. The only way to free the person from being a zombie is to sprinkle some salt on them. Essentially, the fear was less of the zombie itself, and more about becoming a zombie.

Most Famous Myths

In 1962, Narcisse was pronounced dead at the Albert Schweitzer Hospital in Deschapelles, Haiti, after a serious illness. Doctors and his sister confirmed his death, and he was buried the next day. Ten years later, in 1980, a man met Narcisse’s sister at a market and used a childhood nickname only her brother would know. He said he had been awake but unable to move or speak during his funeral, even as he was buried. He claimed a Bokor dug him up that night, put his ti-bon-anj in a bottle, and took him to a sugar plantation. He worked there as a slave with other zombies for two years. He said he got his senses back after someone who didn't know they were zombies fed them salty stew. After that, all the former zombies were enraged by their mistreatement and by having a decade of their lives stolen from them. At that moment, the bokor arrived to overview their work. All the former zombies looked at him with vengance in their eyes and used their farming equipement to kill their oppressor. They hid the corpse and left, hoping to return to their old lives. After hearing this tale, Narcisse's sister cried and invited him back home. Everyone was overjoyed that he returned and he lived the rest of his life with happiness and security.

Cultural Significance

Earlier, it was mentioned that Zombies originated from Haitain Voudou beliefs. Well, Voudou comes from the African slaves who were stolen from their homes and forced to work on plantations in Haiti. Despite coming from very different cultures, they bonded over the shared experience of being stripped of their free will and cultural identity. To cope with this, they combined French Christianity with West African Spirituality to create Voudou. From this perspective, Zombies can be seen as a allegory for slavery. After all, isn't the idea that a soul can be enslaved worse than death? However, the zombies we know in pop culture are very different from their origonal form. This vast change has its origins in the American occupation of Haiti in 1915. Through the lense of racism and western culture, Voudou was portrayed to American audiences as barbaric and evil. In spite of this, Zombies captured the American imagination. They became prevelent in movies, but their Haitain identity and role as victims was slowly replaced by that of a violent monster. Indeed the 1968 film, "Night of the Living Dead" portrayed Zombies as slow-moving, flesh-eating monsters. Since then, Zombies have been defined by their cannibalism. Essentially, their original significance has been erased, and the victim has been transformed into the monster.

Pontianack

Description

The Pontianack is a violent and tragic monster from traditional Indonesian mythology. She has long, black hair, blood red eyes, sharp nails, and is often found in a white dress. She is the ghost of a pregnant woman who faced some sort of male violence. She is a vengeful spirit, nourished by drinking the blood and eating the organs of any violent or unsavory man who crosses her path. She lives in the jungle trees, waiting for prey to come by, using seduction to lure them in, and flying to attack them. However, she is said to emit a high-pitched, eerie laughter that serves as a warning. If the sound is loud, she is far away, but if it is soft, she is dangerously close. However, a way to defeat the Pontianack would be to drive a nail or stake into the back of their neck. This transforms the Pontianack from a vengeful ghost into a submissive, quiet wife. Removing the nail or stake reverts them back to their ghostly form.

Most famous Myth

The legend of the Pontianack is old and popular. Due to that, there are stories from traditional and modern Malay culture. A story from the traditional Malay culture would be the tale of Sari. Sari was a young woman who lived who lived in a small riverside village surrounded by coconut palms and dense jungles. She was a kind and social woman who always smiled and had bright eyes. She married a fisherman and slowly began to change. Her smile slowly faded away and the light in her eyes dimmed. She began to stay indoors more often and when she did leave the house, everyone pretended not to see the bruises on her body. The months went by and Sari became pregnant. Yet there was a massive storm the night she gave birth and the midwife could not come to help. As a result, both Sari and the baby died. For several nights, everyone complained of strange noises that prevented them from sleeping. Fed up with the situation, a hunter named Rahman decided to walk around the village and nearby jungle at night, hoping to fix the problem. One night, he was walking around with a spear and lantern when he heard a loud, eerie laugh coming from the jungle. Thinking that this was the source of the problem, he followed the noise, arriving at the edge of the jungle. However, after several tense minutes, he could no longer hear the noise. Instead, he saw a gorgeous woman with long black hair and a white dress. However, he was no fool. He knew that he was being tricked by a Pontianack. So, he pretended to play along. When the Pontianack tried to lure him into the jungle, he quietly followed her until he was close enough to stab her in the back of the neck with his spear. She reverted back to her human form and the village accepted her as she was.

Meanwhile, a story from the modern day Malay culture would be the tale of Lingggi. A teenage boy stayed with extended family in the rural town of Linggi where there were only moonlit roads and jungle. His relatives, warned him to never go out after dark, always lock the backdoor, and to keep a machete besides it. One night, a distressed woman arrived. She claimed her car broke down on the highway and her husband waited inside the vehicle. She came looking for help and the relatives turned pale. Immediately, men armed themselves with machetes and bamboo poles. They marched as a group to the highway. The teenager followed. They found the car on a dim stretch of road. The woman’s husband lay dead with a young woman sitting next to his body. She carved into his body with her fingernails and began feasting on his organs. Everyone screamed at this sight and she looked up, allowing everyone to see her clearly. She had long black hair, demonic, red eyes, a blood-stained white dress, and a mouth covered in blood. The teenage boy began to recite Qur'anic verses. The woman paused and looked up with mournful, scared eyes. One of the men charged at her with a machete. He brutally hit her shoulder and she shrieked in pain. Once the man removed his machete, the woman fled, dissapearing into the night. The villagers stood silently, not sure of what to do. However, the victim's wife sprang into action. She called an ambulance, hoping to save her husbands life. Unfortunately, it was too late. The villagers helped her fix her car and she returned home. The teenage boy dreamed of that night for many years to come. To this day, he wonders if he witnessed a murderous monster or a vigilant hero.

Cultural Significance

The Pontianack was, and still is, important to Malaysians because of what she represents. In the past, she was a fear-inducing side character for the hero to overcome. Her role in these stories reflected traditional beliefs about morality, respect for women, and the dangers of mistreating others. Essentially, a manifestation of womens vulnerability in childbirth and misogony-derived abuse. In the modern day, she is commonly found in Malay horror movies and urban legends. In this context, she has a sympathetic/vigilante role, and is ocassionally the main character. Essentially, this reinterpritation of her character reflects societys' changing ideas of gender and justice.

Dokkaebi

Description

Dokkaeibi are troll-like creatures from Korean folklore. They have horns, pointed ears, sharp claws, pronounced teeeth, bright red eyes, red or blue skin and wear traditional Korean clothing. They often carry a club known as “dokkaebi bangmangi,” which they use to create gold and control the night. They can be both good and evil. They can bring great fortunes, good harvests, and protection against evil spirits. However, they can also bring misfortune, fires, and spread diseases. Yet at the end of the day, they are mischevious creatures. They can shapeshift into a human form and use it to pursue their love games and challenges. Their favorite game is ssireum (traditional Korean wrestling). They challenge passersby to a match, granting the winner gold and often causing mischief to the loser. However, they are also very gullible and are usually outsmarted by the humans they try to trick. They are created when human blood stains an inanimate object.

Most famous Myth

There was once a woman who lived at the edge of the village, where the Dokkaebi commonly visited. Normally, she viewed them as pests and would chase them away with her broom. However, her husband had recently died and she was in desperate need of money. So, she decided to use the Dokkaebi to her advantage. She used the last of her funds to buy some buckwheat. She turned it into jelly (the Dokkaebi's favorite treat) and invited one inside. She gave him the jelly and began to chat with him. They got along pretty well and as the days went by, became good friends. Eventually, the woman told the Dokkaebi of her struggles. Pitying her, it decided to help by giving her jewels and gold. Having finally gotten what she wanted, the woman quickly became annoyed by the Dokkaebi. She smeared her gate with horse blood and stuck the head of a horse ontop of it. The Dokkaebi, being deathly afraid of horses, never went back to her home and the woman lived comfortably for the rest of her days.

Cultural Significance

The dual nature of the Dokkaebi are a reflection of lifes uncertainty in tradition korean culture. Similar to the Dokkaeibi's unpredictable behavior, Koreas Agricultural Society could be disastrous or profitable based on chance. Also, stories with Dokkaebi tend to punish greedy characters while rewarding those who outwit the demons. This reflects the cultures reverence for confusian values and cleverness. The punishment of greedy characters reflects Confucian values because it paints selfishness, a trait that goes against Confusionism, in a negative light. Meanwhile, the emphasis of cleverness reflects values of intelligence because it rewards those who outwit Dokkaebi by giving them good fortune or banishing their misfortune.

Kikimora

Description

A Kikimora is a housespirit or nature spirit from slavic cultures. The housespirit Kikimoras are depicted as short women with chicken beaks and legs. They tend to influence the household's well-being or misfortune. If household members appeased a Kikimora, she would help with work and ensure that they stayed happy, healthy, and wealth. To appease her, one must keep the house clean or leave offerings of food. However if household members displeased a Kikimora, she would hide their belongings or knock over household items. Yet, if she felt neglected or enraged she would create discord among family members, and bring illness, nightmares/sleep paralysis, or misfortune to the family. Meanwhile, the nature spirit Kikimoras reside in the swamp. They are depicted the same way as housespirit Kikimoras but are covered in mud, moss, and algae. They are far more manevolant, luring travelers to the swamps and drowning them. They are also well known to kidnap children, create illusions, and shape-shifting into animals or elements (mist, wind, ectra). Fortunatly, these Kikimoras can also be driven away through folk rituals and charms.

Most famous Myth

There was once a spinster in rural Poland named Zofia. Like most folks of this time and place, she lived a hard life that broke her spirit down bit by bit. Overworked and bitter, Zofia let her spindle gather dust and never did the customary evening prayer. Yet after a few months, she feared that her mind was turning into a tangled up string. Where she was once orderly and neat, she was messy and forgetful. Her friendships had become bitter, strained by petty disputes. At night, frequent nightmares choked the sleep out of her. Fortunatly, a travelling folk healer stopped by her village and gave her some advice. He told her to scrub the home with birch branches dipped in holy water. She did as told and saw a Kikimora come out of the shadows of her home, howling in pain. Enraged, she grabbed her broom and chased it out of her home. She thought the problem was solved, but every day she would catch the Kikimora trying to return. At first, she just chased it out with her broom but quickly realized that there was a better way to resolve the issue. Instead, she offered bread at her doorstep and waited for the Kikimora to arrive. Once the creature came, she grabbed the bread and held it high above her head. The Kikimora struggled to reach it and Zofia offered her a deal. She said that she would leave the Kikimora some bread everyday, if the creature helped her with the spinning and keeping the house clean. The Kikimora agreed and the two formed a happy alliance that kept them satisfied for the rest of their lives.

Cultural Significance

Kikimora's role as protectors and malevolent spirit reflects the responsabilities of traditional Slavic households and the dangers of nature. Slavic people's respect and fear of household Kikimora's ensured that women kept the house clean, men were not lazy or abusive, and that children went to bed on time. This ensured that everyone did their job in the house which, in turn, kept the family unit healthy and happy. Meanwhile, Slavic people's fears of Swamp Kikimora's taught communities to stay safe by avoiding large bodies of water. Yet an important thing to mention about Kikimora's are their pagan roots. In pre-christian times, she was viewed as an as an ancestral guardian and was called on during rituals around the stove to ensure fertility and prosperity. Yet once Christianity became the dominant religion, many of the old slavic gods or spiritual elements were forgotten or demonized. Kikimora's are no exception to this. They were reimagined as fallen angels or cursed souls being punished for their sins. This reimagined role most likely happened because of how useful it was to Slavic households. After all, it helped maintain order and discipline while explaining things such as night terrors, missing items, deaths of women and children, and strange noises or occurrences around the home.

Selkie

Description

Selkies are shape-shifting creatures from the British isles. They are shape-shifters who transform between seal and human form by removing their pelts. They usually appear on land, transforming into their human form to dance or interact with humans. Stories with Selkies are romantic, but go about differently for men and women. Female Selkie stories usually involve a man stealing their pelts, hiding them, and marrying the women. In these stories, Selkies learn to accept their new domestic life but miss their old lives in the sea. However, she finds the pelt and returns to the ocean. She either abandons her family or takes them with her, drowning them in the water. Meanwhile, male Selkie stories involve the Selkie using a seduction spell to woo lonely human women. Once the woman bears a child, the Selkie takes the child to the ocean and raises them as Selkies. However, the children born of Selkies and humans have their own stories. They are said to have webbed toes/fingers, green skin, and to smell of saltwater. By nature, they are stuck between two worlds, that of the land and the sea. To make matters worse, they often times face ostracization due to their Selkie heritage and feel out out of place in human society.

Cultural Significance

Selkie tales have their roots in Celtic, coastal cultures. They emerged from the imaginations of ancient people as they interacted with the ocean or as they interacted with Sami kayakers who wore clothes made of seal skin. Selkie myths served as explanations for mysterious disappearances, unknown parentage, or the unpredictable nature of the ocean. They also symbolized the transformative power of nature, since the sea was both a source of livelihood and danger for these people.
